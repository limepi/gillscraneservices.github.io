What are the parts and components of a bulkhead?

In saltwater environments, especially in coastal areas of Louisiana and Mississippi, bulkhead construction faces unique challenges due to the harsh conditions. The components and materials used need to withstand corrosion, high salinity, and the dynamic forces of tides and waves. Here’s a detailed look at the components of a bulkhead specifically for saltwater environments in these regions.

Sheet Piles

Vertical planks or sheets driven into the ground to form the primary barrier against water. Often made of steel, vinyl, or fiberglass to resist corrosion.

Wales

Horizontal beams that provide structural support to the sheet piles, often made of treated wood, steel, or composite materials.

Tie Rods

Rods or cables that connect the bulkhead to anchors, providing stability. Made of high-strength steel with protective coatings.

Anchors

Buried components, often concrete or steel, that tie rods connect to, providing stability by counteracting forces.

Cap

Horizontal beam placed on top of the sheet piles for structural integrity and a finished appearance, often made of concrete or treated wood.

Deadmen

Large buried objects, such as concrete or timber, that act as anchors for tie rods, providing resistance to pulling forces.

Backfill

Material placed behind the bulkhead, like soil or gravel, providing support and stability to the structure.

Drainage System

Components like weep holes or pipes that allow water to escape from behind the bulkhead, reducing hydrostatic pressure.

Splash Apron

Surface at the base of the bulkhead designed to prevent erosion from waves or water runoff, often made of concrete or rock riprap.

Bulkhead Considerations for Louisiana and Mississippi Saltwater Environments:

  • Corrosion Resistance: The high salinity in these coastal and marshy areas accelerates the corrosion of metal components. Using materials like vinyl, fiberglass, treated lumber and treated steel with protective coatings is crucial.
  • Marine Life: Marine borers and other organisms can deteriorate wooden components, so pressure-treated or composite materials are preferred.
  • Wave and Tidal Forces: Bulkheads are designed to withstand the dynamic forces of waves and tides, which are significant in these regions. No design can handle all storms, but it is important to choose strong construction techniques from a reliable pile-driving and bulkhead company. It is important to account for higher tides and possible storm surges from potential hurricane impacts.
  • Sediment Movement: Coastal areas in Louisiana and Mississippi often experience significant sediment movement. Bulkhead designs must consider sedimentation patterns to ensure long-term stability.
